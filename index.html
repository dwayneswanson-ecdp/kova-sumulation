Group 1
Executive Leadership Team
Decision Makers
Your Core Tension You have invested €18M and 18 months into this launch. Charité Berlin has a public press event in 48 hours with the Senator for Health attending. But a critical patient safety bug has been confirmed. You must decide — and defend — your decision.
Present the Following:
  • →
    Launch decision: Do you launch, delay, or launch with restrictions? Give clear reasoning using the information from the briefings.
  • →
    Client management: What do you say to Thomas Richter at Charité Berlin, and when? Who makes that call?
  • →
    Investor communication: How do you manage the Series B investor who is flying in for the press event?
  • →
    Trade-off: What are you prioritising — reputation, revenue, safety, or staff? Be honest about the trade-offs.
  • →
    Follow-up actions: What are the three most important decisions that must be made in the next 12 hours?
Group 2
Line Management Team
People First
Your Core Tension Your team found the bug. They are exhausted, and one person has already resigned. You need to protect your people while delivering a solution that leadership can actually act on. You are the bridge between the engineers and the boardroom.
Present the Following:
  • →
    Workload assessment: Based on what you heard, what is realistic to fix in 48 hours versus 3 weeks? Be specific.
  • →
    Team protection: What immediate actions do you take to prevent further resignations? What do you say to Lena Brandt today?
  • →
    Escalation: Marcus escalated but felt pressure to launch with a disclaimer. Was that the right move? What would you have done differently?
  • →
    Accountability: Who in the management chain failed here, and at what point? How do you address that without losing trust?
  • →
    Recovery plan: What does a realistic 3-week fix timeline look like without burning out the remaining team?
Group 3
Human Resources
Culture & Wellbeing
Your Core Tension Sara Osei has one resignation letter and two more people considering leaving. The team feels they were ignored in February when they raised concerns. HR must now act — but also advise leadership on what this crisis reveals about KOVA's culture.
Present the Following:
  • →
    Immediate response: What do you do in the next 24 hours to stabilise morale? Who do you speak to first and what do you say?
  • →
    The resignation: How do you handle the formal resignation letter? Do you try to retain this person, and if so, how?
  • →
    Culture diagnosis: Sara said concerns were raised in February and ignored. What does this tell you about how KOVA handles feedback and accountability?
  • →
    Leadership message: What do you recommend Dr. Jana Kovač communicates to the entire company today — and how?
  • →
    Long-term: What policy or process change would prevent this situation from repeating?
Group 4
Client Representative — Charité Berlin
External Stakeholder
Your Core Tension You are Thomas Richter's team at Charité Berlin. You have the Senator for Health confirmed, journalists invited, and a public statement already made about this partnership. You have not heard from KOVA in 24 hours. You are about to find out why.
Present the Following:
  • →
    Your position: When Jana calls, what is your opening position? Are you angry, understanding, or something in between? Why?
  • →
    Decision point: If KOVA asks to delay by 3 weeks, what conditions do you set before agreeing? What would make you walk away entirely?
  • →
    The press event: The Senator is confirmed and journalists are invited. How do you manage this publicly? Do you cancel, postpone, or reframe the event?
  • →
    Trust: KOVA did not call you for 24 hours. How does this affect your view of the partnership going forward?
  • →
    Patient safety: As a hospital, how do you weigh the safety risk against the operational disruption of a delay?
